REVIEWER: Jeff McGovern

NAME: G. Brothers Smokehouse

ADDRESS: 885 Foothill Blvd.

CITY: San Luis Obispo, CA

FOOD: 2

SERVICE: 4

VENUE: 2.5

RATING: 3

WRITTEN REVIEW: 

The food at G. Brothers Smokehouse is mediocre and somewhat expensive, but edible and filling. A Texas style brisket sandwich with a side of fries will cost about $9. The brisket is dry, tough, and lacking any discernible flavor. It kind of feels like chewing paper. In order to combat the dryness, I smothered the brisket in their homemade Texas BBQ sauce, which helped with the dry chewiness and added a bit of flavor. The sauce doesn't have much to it, except for a little mesquite flavor and some kick. The bread surrounding the brisket is a standard french roll, buttered and lightly grilled on the inside. The fries are most likely pre-bought and frozen, but are crispy without being dry and lightly seasoned with a reasonably good seasoning. The meal might satisfy someone who's hungry, but there isn't much else to say about it. If it were $2-3 cheaper, it might be worth the money. The beer selection is better than many common restaurants, serving staples like Stone IPA and Firestone DBA at a reasonable $3.75 (though my receipt said I was charged for a Coors Light, so that price may be wrong). There are many options for food, but not so many that the menu is confusing. Breakfast options exist, but only until 11 AM, which seems early for breakfast in a college town.

Ordering food at G. Brothers Smokehouse is straightforward and easy. Underneath the large wall-mounted menu in the main area, there is a front desk with a register and an attendant where you order food. The attendant charges for your food and someone will bring your food to you at your seat. The process doesn't take too long and food arrives in a reasonable amount of time. Self-serve condiments are on one side of the relatively small restaurant and, in addition to ketchup and mustard, they offer their own BBQ sauces and ranch dressing.

G. Brothers Smokehouse has a mom and pop, country feel to it, almost to the point that it's in your face. The building is small, quaint, has a little brick patio, and has pieces of what appear to be barn wood tacked onto the walls with that Old West style font engraved and painted into it. There's an amount of clutter, what with a broken vending machine (containing at least one cheese pastry), a coin-operated carousel horse that one would expect outside of a grocery store, and a general feeling that you've walked into a San Luis Obispo tourist attraction. There are two TVs, an 11 year old CRT that plays 60 minutes and a flat screen playing a Texas version of Kitchen Nightmares, which made some of us lose our appetite when they revealed the back kitchen. Showing a reality show that's critical of small, locally owned restaurants may not be the right environment for eating food at a place like G. Brothers. Seating appears to specifically target 6 person groups, with all but one section of tables being a group of three tables, three chairs, and either three more chairs or bench seating. The seating might've been awkward if the restaurant was packed, but fortunately it wasn't. The tables look like they were pulled out of the bed of a truck that morning and are thrown back into the truck every night, adding dark scuffs and tears in the vinyl coverings. The main seating area appears to be an addition to the main building, built much like a sunroom and adding to the Texan decor. Somehow, this place stays open from 7:30 AM until 9 PM. 

Eating at G. Brothers Smokehouse is a generally nonnegative experience. The food is lacking and slightly overpriced for what you get, but it will likely be a moderately filling meal. The ease of ordering and paying for (albeit mediocre) food along with the laid back atmosphere makes G. Brothers Smokehouse an acceptable place to eat.
